Spaced Repetition: The Review Schedule That Makes Memory Stick

Cram five hours on Sunday and you'll know the material Monday. By Friday, most of it is gone — and that's not a personal failing, it's the forgetting curve, mapped by Hermann Ebbinghaus in the 1880s and replicated ever since. Memory decays on a predictable schedule. The good news hiding in that sentence: predictable things can be scheduled against.

The core idea in one paragraph

Each time you successfully recall something just as you're about to forget it, the memory comes back stronger and decays slower. So instead of massing your reviews (cramming), you space them at growing intervals — catch the memory at each near-forgetting point, and within a handful of reviews the decay curve flattens to nearly nothing. Same total study time, radically different retention. In research comparisons, spaced study consistently crushes massed study, and the advantage grows with time-to-exam.

The practical schedule

Forget the perfect algorithm — the wins come from any reasonable spacing. Our default for coursework:

  • Review 1: same evening as the class or first study session (10 minutes, blank-page dump or flashcards).
  • Review 2: 2–3 days later.
  • Review 3: one week later.
  • Review 4: two to three weeks later.
  • Review 5: monthly until the final.

Every review is retrieval, never re-reading — test yourself, check, move on. A topic that survives two clean reviews gets promoted to longer intervals; a topic you fumble drops back to the start. That's the entire system.

App or paper? Both work

Apps (Anki and its cousins) automate the scheduling perfectly and shine for high-volume memorization — languages, anatomy, law, bar and board exams. The cost is setup discipline and the temptation to spend more time formatting cards than recalling them. Paper (the Leitner box: five sections, cards move forward on success and back to the front on failure) teaches the same principle with zero screens, and works beautifully for younger students. We genuinely don't care which one a student uses — we care that reviews actually happen on schedule, which is a calendar problem, not a technology problem.

Fitting it into a real semester

The objection we hear weekly: "I don't have time to review old material — I'm drowning in new material." The arithmetic says otherwise. Spaced reviews are short — 10–15 minutes per course, two or three times a week — because you're maintaining memories, not rebuilding them. Cramming is what's expensive: every massed session is a full reconstruction of material your calendar let die. Students who switch report the strangest sensation around week six: finals period arrives and there's nothing to cram — they already know it. The panic-shaped hole in their schedule becomes sleep.

The compound-interest framing

Cramming is renting knowledge — pay in full, keep it for days. Spacing is buying it in installments — smaller payments, permanent ownership. For any subject that builds on itself (math, languages, sciences — that is, most subjects), the material you own keeps paying dividends in every later chapter that assumes it.
